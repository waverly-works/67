A design experiment

The number
that became
a gesture.

You don't need a punchline. You don't need context. You just move your hands up and down and everyone laughs. (Or groans in exasperation.)

67 is a meme that lives in the body. It bypasses language entirely — you see the numbers next to each other, or you raise one hand and lower the other, and something electric passes through a room. No translation required. No cultural footnote. Just collective recognition of something absurd that we, as a society, decided was hilarious.

As a designer, I kept coming back to that moment of shared understanding. How does a pair of mundane, unremarkable digits acquire meaning? Not through utility, not through beauty, but through pure social agreement. We assigned the joke. We spread it. We kept it alive with nothing more than our hands and social media.

67 Finder started as a conversation with my roommates: I was with them in Chinatown, Boston. We wanted Molly Tea. Lo and behold, Molly Tea just so happens to be 67 Beach St. When we realized, we cackled. I can't forget the simple joy this brought us: a set of numbers that once meant nothing when paired together, now a global meme, mixed with our yummy drinks.

What happens when you take that absurd energy and point it at something completely literal? Streets. Avenues. Addresses. The number 67 is everywhere — embossed on doorframes, painted on curbs, printed on maps — blissfully unaware that it has become a punchline. This site is about that gap. The space between the mundane and the meme. Between the address and the joke.
